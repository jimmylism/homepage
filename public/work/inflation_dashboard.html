01
Supply-Chain Seizure
Global logistics networks contracted sharply through 2020–2021. Port congestion, semiconductor shortages, and freight cost spikes transmitted directly into consumer prices across all 20 economies — before central banks had time to act.
02
Commodity Shock of 2022
Crude oil surged past $130/bbl and global food price indices rose 25% above pre-pandemic baselines. Geopolitical volatility amplified energy market disruption, pushing headline inflation to multi-decade highs across all income groups.
03
Policy Divergence
Central banks responded at vastly different speeds. The UK and US tightened aggressively; Japan and the EU moved cautiously. This divergence produced markedly different inflation trajectories even among similarly exposed economies.

Key Findings

What the Data Tells Us

Three structural takeaways for policymakers, investors, and economists drawn from the full cross-national dataset.

Signal
Supply chains led, policy followed
The supply-chain pressure index shows a statistically significant positive association with consumer price inflation — most pronounced during 2021–2022. The transmission preceded central-bank responses by an average of two to three quarters.
Watch
Food price pass-through stays elevated
The global food price index has not reverted to pre-COVID levels even as headline inflation moderated. Emerging markets with higher food-basket weights — IND, ZAF, SAU — face structurally stickier inflation as a result.
Context
Reaction speed shaped outcomes
GBR and KOR tightened early and saw faster moderation. CAN and AUS moved later and experienced higher peak inflation relative to historical norms. The data confirms reaction speed, not just magnitude, drove differentiated outcomes.
